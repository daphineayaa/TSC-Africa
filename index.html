Our Mission

TSCA is committed to reducing mortality and improving the quality of life for sickle cell warriors through dedicated community-based interventions.

We are a youth-led public health initiative addressing the staggering burden of Sickle Cell Disease in vulnerable communities.

Sickle Cell FAQs

Answers to the most common questions about Sickle Cell Disease (SCD).

What is Sickle Cell Disease (SCD)?

Sickle Cell Disease is an inherited blood disorder where red blood cells become rigid, sticky, and sickle-shaped due to abnormal hemoglobin (HbS). These cells block blood flow and break down early, causing anemia and episodes of pain.

What are the symptoms of SCD?

Common symptoms include chronic anemia, pain crises, swelling in hands/feet, frequent infections, delayed growth, jaundice, and organ damage.

How is Sickle Cell Disease inherited?

SCD is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern. A child must inherit two sickle cell genes (one from each parent) to have the disease. Carrier parents (AS + AS) have:

Can SCD be cured?

The only potential cure is a bone marrow (stem-cell) transplant, most successful in children. New gene therapies (such as CRISPR-based treatments like Casgevy) offer additional hope.

How is SCD managed?

Management includes hydroxyurea, crisis pain treatment, folic acid supplementation, blood transfusions, antibiotics for children, vaccinations, and new drugs such as L-glutamine and crizanlizumab.

Who is most affected by Sickle Cell Disease?

SCD mainly affects people of African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and Indian descent. Sub-Saharan Africa carries the highest burden with millions of affected individuals.

How can SCD be prevented?

Prevention involves carrier screening, genetic counseling, newborn screening, hydroxyurea therapy from 9 months, vaccinations, and infection prevention.
